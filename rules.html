The Basics: How Do You Play Baccarat?

While there can be as many as 10 players at a baccarat table at any time, the game is straightforward and easy to follow. Your goal as a player is to correctly guess which hand's total value will be closest to nine before the cards are dealt.

Here's how a round normally plays out:

  • All players place their bets on one of three outcomes — the Player hand, the Banker hand, or a Tie. Neither hand represents the guest or the house.
  • Once all bets are in, the dealer deals two cards to each hand, alternating: Player, Banker, Player, Banker.
  • If either hand has a total value of eight or nine (a "natural"), both hands stand and the round ends immediately. Standing means no further cards are drawn.
  • If neither hand has a natural, the drawing rules below determine whether one or both hands receive a third card. No hand ever holds more than three cards.
  • Whichever hand ends up closest to nine wins, and bets are paid out. If both hands finish with the same total, the Tie bet wins — and bets placed on Player or Banker are returned (a push), so you don't lose them.

How Do You Add a Hand's Value?

To determine the total value of a hand, simply add the values of its cards together. The values vary by the type of card:

  • Numbered cards: all cards numbered two through nine are worth their face value.
  • Tens and face cards: kings, queens, jacks and tens are all worth zero points.
  • Aces: all aces equal one point.

If the total exceeds nine, the first digit gets dropped. For example, a hand of 7 + 5 totals 12, which counts as two points. This means a hand can never "bust" — every total lands between zero and nine.

When Do Participants Draw a Third Card?

Each participant follows different rules for when they must draw a third card. These rules are automatic — neither the Player nor the Banker chooses whether to draw.

The Player acts first. The Player draws a third card if the first two cards total zero to five, and stands on six or seven. (Remember: totals of eight or nine are naturals, and the round has already ended.)

The Banker's rules depend on whether the Player drew a third card.

If the Player stood (with a total of six or seven), the Banker follows the same simple rule as the Player: draw on zero to five, stand on six or seven.

If the Player drew a third card, the Banker draws on a total of zero to two, and otherwise follows this table based on the value of the Player's third card:

  • Three: the Banker draws unless the Player's third card is an eight.
  • Four: the Banker draws if the Player's third card is two through seven.
  • Five: the Banker draws if the Player's third card is four through seven.
  • Six: the Banker draws only if the Player's third card is a six or seven.
  • Seven: the Banker always stands.

The Player's third-card value is its baccarat value: tens and face cards count as zero, aces as one. For example, a Banker showing three draws against a Player's third-card king (value zero), since eight is the only exception.

Who Should You Bet on in Baccarat?

Unlike in other table games, you don't play against the dealer in baccarat. You bet on the outcome of each round instead. Your options include:

  • The Banker (pays 0.95:1): because the Banker acts last and reacts to the Player's third card, this hand wins slightly more often (45.860%). Betting on the Banker is typically the best baccarat strategy for new players. The 5% commission on winnings is what keeps the house edge intact.
  • The Player (pays 1:1): the Player hand wins slightly less often than the Banker (44.625%), but winning bets are paid in full with no commission.
  • Tie (pays 8:1): you can bet that both hands will finish with the same value. The higher payout reflects how rare this outcome is (9.516%) — it carries the highest house edge of the three bets. Remember that on a tie, Player and Banker bets are simply returned.

3 Classic Baccarat Betting Systems

Want to structure your play like the regulars do? These three well-known systems give your session a plan. Baccaelite.com is the perfect place to try them: the coins are virtual, so you can test any system for as long as you like at zero risk.

1. Paroli

The Paroli system rides winning streaks: you double your bet after each win, and reset after three consecutive wins or any loss. For example, bet $1 and win — your next bet is $2, then $4. After the third win (or the first loss), you return to $1. Because you only ever risk increasing amounts with money you just won, it's one of the lowest-risk systems and a good starting point.

2. Fibonacci

This system follows the famous Fibonacci sequence, where each number is the sum of the two before it: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34… Your bets follow the same ladder: start at $1, and after each loss move one step up the sequence ($1, $1, $2, $3, $5…). After a win, move two steps back down. Set a maximum step in advance and restart from the beginning if you reach it, so the ladder never runs away from your bankroll.

3. Martingale

The Martingale is the oldest and most straightforward system: double your bet after every loss, so that a single win recovers all previous losses plus one unit of profit. Start at $1, lose, and bet $2; lose again and bet $4, and so on — until a win resets you to $1. It requires discipline and a deep bankroll, because losing streaks make the bets grow very fast. We recommend practicing it here extensively before ever considering it in a real casino.

An honest note: no betting system changes the odds of the game — the house edge is the same on every hand regardless of how you size your bets. Systems manage the rhythm of your bankroll, not the probabilities. That's exactly why a free simulator is the ideal place to explore them.
